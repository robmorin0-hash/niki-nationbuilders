Emergency Water Supply: Practical Guide for Survival and Hygiene

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: water-purification-sanitation-systems-depth.

In emergency scenarios, water is the most critical resource for survival. The minimum water requirement for basic survival is 5–7.5 liters per person per day, but higher volumes are needed to maintain hygiene and prevent disease. Understanding these thresholds and the methods to purify water can mean the difference between life and death in a crisis.

Minimum Water Requirements

The physiological and humanitarian standards for water consumption vary depending on the situation. For survival minimums with no sanitation or cooking, 1–3 liters per person per day is sufficient, but this level risks rapid disease spread. Basic survival, requiring drinking and minimal hygiene, demands 5–7.5 liters. In initial emergency response, 7.5–15 liters per day is the target to prevent dehydration and hygiene collapse. Maintaining basic hygiene requires 15–20 liters, while a minimal domestic supply is 50 liters. European/North American averages are 150–300 liters, but these are not applicable in collapse scenarios.

The immediate priority is ensuring 7.5–15 liters per day. Below 5 liters, disease burden and hygiene failure escalate rapidly. In disaster zones, securing this volume is critical to prevent outbreaks of waterborne illnesses and ensure basic sanitation.

Emergency Chlorination

Chlorination is a vital method to disinfect water in emergencies. For sodium dichloroisocyanurate (NaDCC) tablets (33 mg/tablet, ~67% available Cl):

  • Clear water: 1 tablet per 20 liters; allow 30 minutes contact time before drinking.
  • Cloudy/turbid water: 2 tablets per 20 liters; same contact time.
  • Very turbid water: pre-filter through cloth layers, then dose as cloudy water.

For household bleach (5% NaOCl, ~50,000 mg/L available Cl):

  • Clear water: 2 drops (0.1 mL) per liter = ~5 mg/L; contact time 30 minutes.
  • Cloudy water: 4 drops per liter.
  • Scale: 10 mL bleach per 1,000 liters clear water = 0.5 mg/L.

    • Verify residual chlorine after 30 minutes: should detect 0.2–0.5 mg/L free Cl. If not detectable, add more.

Always test for residual chlorine to ensure effective disinfection.

Improvised Filtration

A multi-layer filter made from available materials can improve water quality by removing turbidity and reducing pathogens, but it cannot guarantee safe drinking water. Always follow filtration with boiling or chemical disinfection.

Layer sequence (top to bottom in a container with a drainage hole): 1. Coarse grass or straw (removes large debris). 2. Coarse gravel (10–20 mm, 10 cm layer). 3. Fine gravel or coarse sand (5–10 mm, 5 cm). 4. Clean coarse sand (10 cm). 5. Crushed charcoal (wood charcoal, not lighter fluid, 5 cm). 6. Fine clean sand (15 cm). 7. Cloth or fine moss layer (bottom).

This setup removes suspended solids, some bacteria (via mechanical filtration), and chemicals/taste/odor (via charcoal adsorption). However, it does not reliably remove dissolved chemicals (nitrates, heavy metals), viruses, or all bacteria. Boiling is essential after filtration to ensure safety.

Hollow Fiber and LifeStraw-Type Filters

Commercial hollow-fiber ultrafiltration (UF) systems, such as Sawyer PointONE or LifeStraw, use membranes with pore sizes 0.1–0.2 µm to retain bacteria, protozoa, and some viruses. Water flows through the membrane under pressure (suction or gravity).

Critical maintenance: Backflush regularly (ideally after each use) to prevent clogging. A clogged filter may develop flow through cracks or pinholes, bypassing the membrane entirely. If a filter becomes difficult to blow through after extended inactivity, discard it.

Freezing danger: Frozen water expands, rupturing the membrane tubes. A frozen filter is compromised and must be discarded. Mark filters with a bright warning label if used in cold environments.

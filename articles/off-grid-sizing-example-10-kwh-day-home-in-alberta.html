Off-Grid Sizing Example: 10 kWh/Day Home in Alberta

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: solar-pv-systems-design-installation-depth.

Designing an off-grid system for a 10 kWh/day home in Alberta requires balancing seasonal load variations, battery storage, and component sizing to ensure reliability. This guide breaks down the practical steps to size a solar + battery system for a home with moderate heating needs, using real-world data from Alberta’s climate and energy usage patterns.

Designing for Seasonal Variability

The first step is to define the load profile based on seasonal energy use. A 10 kWh/day average assumes moderate heating and limited cooling, but winter months in Alberta can push demand to 15 kWh/day. To account for extended cloudy periods, the system must support 3 days of autonomy without sunlight, requiring a minimum battery capacity of 45 kWh (15 kWh/day × 3 days).

For system reliability, aim for 95% availability, meaning outages are acceptable for about 18 days/year. This targets a 20% state of charge (SOC) threshold for triggering a backup generator. The generator should recharge the battery and support critical loads during extended cloudy weather, ensuring the battery isn’t deeply discharged.

Sizing the Components

PV Array: Matching Winter Demand

Alberta’s worst-case winter solar irradiance is 3.0 PSH (peak sun hours) in December. To meet the 15 kWh/day winter demand, the solar array must generate 6.7 kW minimum (15 kWh / (3.0 PSH × 0.75 efficiency)). A conservative specification of 8 kW accounts for degradation and suboptimal tilt. This is achieved with 20 × 400W monocrystalline panels, arranged in two parallel strings of 10 modules each.

Battery Bank: Storing Winter Energy

The battery must store 45 kWh of usable energy at 80% depth of discharge (DoD), requiring 56 kWh of LFP (lithium iron phosphate) nameplate capacity. A 48V nominal system using 8 × 6 kWh LFP modules (e.g., Linamax) provides flexibility and longevity. At $400/kWh, this costs $22,400.

Charge Controller: Managing Current and Efficiency

The array’s short-circuit current (Isc) is 220 A (2 × 11A per module × 10 modules per string). An MPPT controller must handle 275 A (220 A × 1.25). A 150A dual-MPPT controller (e.g., Victron SmartSolar 150/100 × 2 units) provides sufficient capacity for the 8 kW array.

Inverter: Supporting Peak Loads

The system’s average continuous load is 0.42 kW, but peak loads (e.g., a 2 kW well pump) require a 6 kW surge capacity. A 5 kW continuous, 10 kW peak inverter ensures compatibility with motors and electronics without overloading.

Generator: Backup Power for Extended Outages

A 8 kW backup generator matches the solar array’s nominal capacity and supports full load during extended cloudy weather. At 50% load, it consumes 1 gallon/hour of fuel, requiring ~500 gallons seasonal storage for Alberta’s winter autonomy.

Cost and ROI Analysis

The total system cost ranges from $47,400 to $51,400, including components, installation, and permits. The cost per kWh is $4.70–$5.10, reflecting the high upfront investment in battery storage.

Annual savings from avoiding grid electricity (10 kWh/day × 365 days × $0.15/kWh) and generator fuel (500 gal/year × $2.50/gal) total $1,800. At this rate, the payback period is 26–29 years, assuming utility rates rise 2–3% annually. While the ROI is long-term, the system’s value lies in energy independence and resilience, especially in regions with unreliable grid access.

Gear that Helps

  • An MPPT charge controller
  • A 400W monocrystalline solar panel
  • 6 kWh LFP battery modules
  • A 5 kW continuous, 10 kW peak inverter
  • A 8 kW backup generator

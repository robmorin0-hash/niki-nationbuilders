Battery Management Systems: Practical Guide for Off-Grid Use

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: battery-designs-from-scratch-chemistry-build-depth.

Battery Management Systems (BMS) are critical for maintaining safety, longevity, and efficiency in off-grid energy storage. This guide breaks down three core functions—state-of-charge (SOC) estimation, state-of-health (SOH) tracking, and thermal management—into actionable steps for system design and maintenance.

Coulomb Counting for SOC Estimation

Coulomb counting calculates SOC by integrating current over time. The formula $SOC(t) = \frac{Q(t)}{Q_{\max}} \times 100\%$ shows how stored charge (Q) relates to maximum capacity (Q_max). For example, a 100 Ah battery at 50% SOC (50 Ah stored) charges at 10 A for 4 hours, adding 40 Ah. The new SOC becomes 90% ((50 + 40)/100).

However, this method accumulates errors over time due to sensor drift. To correct this, reset the coulomb counter daily or weekly using open-circuit voltage (OCV). For lithium iron phosphate (LFP) batteries, OCV varies predictably with SOC. A 4S (4-cell) LFP pack’s OCV lookup table is:

| SOC (%) | OCV (V, 4S) | |---------|-------------| | 100 | 14.6 | | 80 | 13.8 | | 60 | 13.4 | | 40 | 13.0 | | 20 | 12.4 | | 0 | 10.0 |

Measure OCV after a 2-hour rest period when the pack equilibrates, then adjust the coulomb counter to match the table. This calibration reduces drift errors and improves accuracy.

State of Health (SOH) Tracking

SOH measures a battery’s remaining capacity relative to its original value. The formula $SOH = \frac{\text{Current Max Capacity}}{\text{Original Capacity}} \times 100\%$ highlights degradation over time. To estimate SOH, fully charge and discharge the battery quarterly at a low C-rate (0.1C). For example, a 100 Ah battery discharged over 8–10 hours at 10 A (0.1C) will show its current capacity. Compare this to the baseline (original capacity). If the measured Ah drops below 80% of the baseline, flag the battery for inspection or replacement.

LFP batteries typically degrade at 2–3% per year during the first 3 years (calendar + cycling effects), slowing to 0.5–1% per year afterward. At 2.5% annual degradation, an LFP battery reaches 80% SOH in 8 years. While still functional for lower-power applications, this marks the end of its "first life" and suggests repurposing or recycling.

Thermal Management in BMS

Temperature control prevents overheating, overcooling, and chemical degradation. Use NTC thermistors (Negative Temperature Coefficient) to monitor temperature. These sensors have a resistance drop of ~−4%/°C, with a typical 10k Ω resistance at 25°C. Measure voltage across a 10k Ω resistor and thermistor in a voltage divider, then calculate temperature using the Steinhart-Hart equation or a lookup table. Thermistors cost $1–5 each.

Implement control logic to disable charging if the battery temperature exceeds 50°C or falls below 0°C (risk of lithium plating at low temps). Similarly, stop discharging if the temperature surpasses 80°C (runaway risk) or drops below −20°C (capacity loss). Log a thermal alert if the temperature gradient (dT/dt) exceeds 2°C/min, which may indicate a short circuit or high-power discharge.

Gear that Helps

  • NTC thermistors (10k Ω @ 25°C, $1–5 each) for temperature sensing
  • A data logger for OCV and temperature monitoring
  • A current sensor/shunt for coulomb counting

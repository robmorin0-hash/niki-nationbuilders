Sedimentation and Coagulation for Water Treatment

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: water-purification-sanitation-systems-depth.

Water purification begins with removing suspended particles through sedimentation and coagulation. These processes target turbidity and pathogens, forming the foundation of any effective treatment system. Understanding their mechanics and practical implementation ensures safe, sustainable water access.

Sedimentation: Letting Particles Settle

Sedimentation relies on gravity to separate particles from water. Larger particles (>50 µm) settle rapidly within minutes, while silt (2–50 µm) takes hours. Clay and colloidal particles (<2 µm) settle too slowly to be practical without intervention. A storage tank or reservoir left undisturbed for 24–48 hours can remove coarse turbidity and reduce pathogen load, but this alone is insufficient for safe drinking water. Sedimentation improves downstream treatment efficiency by pre-concentrating solids, making it a critical first step.

Coagulation-Flocculation: Binding Fine Particles

Colloidal and fine particles (like pathogens) require coagulation to destabilize their electrostatic charge, enabling them to clump into flocs. These flocs then settle faster than individual particles. Coagulants like aluminum sulfate (alum), ferric sulfate, or natural alternatives like *Moringa oleifera* seed powder are used.

Alum (Al₂(SO₄)₃·18H₂O):

  • Dose: 5–50 mg/L (depends on turbidity and alkalinity).
  • Optimal pH: 6.5–8.0; efficiency drops sharply outside this range.
  • Consumes 0.5 mg/L alkalinity (as CaCO₃) per 1 mg/L alum. Add lime if alkalinity <50 mg/L.
  • Residual aluminum: <0.2 mg/L with proper dosing.

Ferric Sulfate:

  • Works across pH 5–9; better in cold water.
  • Produces denser flocs but may stain water if residual iron remains.

Natural Coagulants:

  • *Moringa oleifera* (50–200 mg/L): Food-safe, biodegradable floc; ideal for low-resource settings.
  • Cactus mucilage: Effective as a primary or aid coagulant.

Jar Test: Finding the Right Dose

The jar test determines optimal coagulant dosage for a given water source. Repeat this when water quality changes.

1. Fill six 1-liter jars with raw water. 2. Prepare a 1% alum stock solution (1 g in 100 mL). 3. Add doses of 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 mg/L (0.5–5.0 mL per liter). 4. Rapid mix at 100–150 rpm for 1 minute. 5. Slow mix at 20–30 rpm for 20–30 minutes. 6. Let settle for 30–60 minutes. 7. Measure turbidity in the supernatant. Select the lowest dose achieving <1 NTU. Check pH; it should stay above 6.5.

Treatment Train: Combining Steps for Efficiency

A sequential approach maximizes results: Raw water → Coagulant addition (rapid mix 1 min) → Flocculation (slow mix 20–30 min) → Settling (2–4 hours) → Filtration → Disinfection This order ensures coagulated flocs settle before filtration, reducing load on filters and improving disinfection efficacy.

Upflow Clarifiers: Optimizing Settling

Upflow clarifiers use a "sludge blanket" of settled floc to capture new particles. Water flows upward through the blanket, which self-regulates within a range. Design the upflow velocity to be less than the floc’s settling velocity—typically 0.5–2.0 m/hr for alum. Excess sludge exits the top and is periodically removed.

Gear that Helps

  • A coagulant dosing pump or measuring tool
  • A mixing tank or container for rapid/flocculation stages
  • A settling tank or reservoir (≥24–48 hours retention time)
  • A turbidity meter or NTU measurement tool
  • A pH meter or test kit (for monitoring coagulation efficiency)

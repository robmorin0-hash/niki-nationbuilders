Inverter Selection and Sizing for Off-Grid Systems

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: solar-pv-systems-design-installation-depth.

Inverter selection is critical for ensuring reliable power conversion from DC batteries to AC loads in off-grid systems. Proper sizing prevents overloading, extends equipment lifespan, and maintains stable voltage/frequency. Here’s how to approach this task effectively.

Sizing Approach for Continuous and Peak Loads

To determine the correct inverter size, calculate the continuous and peak power requirements based on average and surge loads. For a 30 kWh/day home with 1.25 kW average load:

  • Continuous rating: Multiply the average load by 1.25 to account for surge headroom (e.g., 1.25 kW × 1.25 = 1.56 kW minimum).
  • Peak rating: Multiply the continuous rating by 3–5 to support motor starting inrush (e.g., 1.56 kW × 3 = 4.7 kW minimum).

    • A practical choice would be a 4–5 kW continuous inverter with an 8–10 kW peak rating. Always verify that the inverter’s peak capacity can handle surges from motors, such as a 2 kW well pump requiring 6 kW for 2 seconds.

Key Features for Off-Grid Inverters

Off-grid inverters must provide stable voltage/frequency regulation without grid dependence. Prioritize these features:

  • Dual-mode operation: Switch between battery power and grid charging (if available) for redundancy.
  • Transfer relay: Ensure automatic, imperceptible switchover (<50 ms) between battery and grid.
  • Input voltage range: Choose an inverter that operates within 40–60 VDC to accommodate battery voltage fluctuations (45V nominal to 55V charged).
  • True sine wave output: Required for sensitive electronics like computers, refrigerators, and medical devices.

Efficiency, Temperature, and Load Considerations

Inverter efficiency varies with load and temperature:

  • Efficiency curve: Peak efficiency (96–99%) occurs at 50–75% rated load. Efficiency drops to 90–94% at light loads (<10% rating) and 94–97% at peak loads (>90% rating).
  • Temperature derating: Efficiency decreases by 0.3% per °C above 25°C ambient. For example, a 5 kW inverter at 45°C ambient would have 93.2% efficiency (95% at 25°C minus 6°C × 0.3%).
  • Cooling: Passive convection suffices for <3 kW inverters; active fans are needed for >3 kW to maintain efficiency.

Gear That Helps

  • An MPPT charge controller
  • A 48V battery system (e.g., lithium or lead-acid)
  • A true sine wave off-grid inverter (4–5 kW continuous / 8–10 kW peak; e.g., Victron MultiPlus-II, Schneider XW Pro, or all-in-one Sol-Ark)
  • A transfer switch for dual-mode (battery/grid) operation
  • A battery management system (BMS)

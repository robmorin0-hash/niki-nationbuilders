Inverter Selection and Sizing for Off-Grid and Grid-Tied Systems

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: solar-pv-systems-design-installation-depth.

Off-grid systems rely on inverters to convert stored DC battery power into usable AC electricity. Proper sizing ensures reliable operation, while features like dual-mode operation and true sine wave output address critical needs like voltage regulation and sensitive appliance compatibility.

Sizing for Off-Grid Inverters

To size an inverter for a 30 kWh/day home (average load 1.25 kW), calculate continuous and peak ratings:

  • Continuous rating: Multiply average load by 1.25 (1.25 kW × 1.25 = 1.56 kW minimum).
  • Peak rating: Multiply continuous rating by 3–5 (1.56 kW × 3 = 4.7 kW minimum).

    • Select an inverter with 4–5 kW continuous capacity and 8–10 kW peak rating to handle motor inrushes (e.g., a 2 kW well pump may require 6 kW startup surge).

Inverters must support 40–60 VDC input to accommodate battery voltage fluctuations (45V nominal to 55V charged). A true sine wave output is mandatory for devices like computers and refrigerators. Dual-mode inverters switch seamlessly between battery and grid power (if available), while a transfer relay ensures <50 ms switchover for uninterrupted loads.

Grid-Tied Inverters: Unidirectional vs. Bidirectional

Unidirectional grid-tied inverters convert solar DC to AC for home use or grid export. They include anti-islanding protection to disconnect within 0.5 seconds if the grid fails, complying with IEEE 1547 and UL 1741 standards. Detection methods include passive voltage/frequency shifts or active harmonic injection.

Bidirectional grid-tied inverters (emerging technology) integrate batteries to store solar energy and discharge during peak hours or outages. These systems require a battery management system, transfer switches, and grid voltage monitoring. Examples include Tesla Powerwall (proprietary) and Enphase IQ batteries.

Inverter Efficiency and Environmental Factors

Efficiency varies with load:

  • Peak efficiency: 96–99% at 50–75% rated load.
  • Light load: 90–94% (idle losses).
  • Peak load: 94–97% (thermal throttling).

Temperature derating reduces efficiency by 0.3% per °C above 25°C ambient. For example, a 5 kW inverter at 45°C ambient achieves 93.2% efficiency (95% at 25°C minus 6°C × 0.3%). Passive cooling suffices for <3 kW systems; active fans are needed for higher capacities.

Switching frequency (15–20 kHz) affects electromagnetic interference (EMI). Transformer-less designs offer 98%+ efficiency but lack galvanic isolation, which may violate safety codes. Transformer-based inverters provide 95–97% efficiency with isolation, though they are bulkier.

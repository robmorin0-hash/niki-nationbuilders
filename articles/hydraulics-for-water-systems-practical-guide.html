Hydraulics for Water Systems: Practical Guide

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: water-purification-sanitation-systems-depth.

Hydraulic calculations are essential for designing reliable off-grid water systems. Whether sizing pipes, selecting pumps, or planning storage, understanding principles like the Hazen-Williams equation, pressure head, and system efficiency ensures safe, sustainable water delivery. This guide provides actionable steps for each key area.

Pipe Sizing with the Hazen-Williams Equation

The Hazen-Williams equation is the most practical method for determining pipe diameter based on flow rate, head loss, and material properties. For a circular pipe flowing full, the formula for diameter is:

$$D = \left[ \frac{Q}{0.2785 \times C \times S^{0.54}} \right]^{1/2.63}$$

Where:

  • $Q$ = flow rate in m³/s
  • $C$ = Hazen-Williams roughness coefficient (e.g., 150 for PVC)
  • $S$ = hydraulic gradient (head loss per unit length, e.g., 0.03 m/m for 15 m over 500 m)

Example: A village needs 10 L/s (0.010 m³/s) through 500 m of PVC pipe with a 15 m head loss. Using $C = 150$ and $S = 0.03$, the calculation yields a required diameter of ~85.6 mm. Standard PVC sizes would be 90 mm or 100 mm.

Key considerations:

  • Use $R = D/4$ for circular pipes.
  • Adjust $C$ values based on material (e.g., 140 for HDPE, 80–100 for old cast iron).
  • Always select the next standard size to account for friction losses and future demand.

Elevated Storage and Gravity Head

Gravity-fed systems are the most reliable and low-maintenance option. Pressure is determined by elevation: 1 m of head ≈ 0.1 bar. Minimum pressure requirements are:

  • 1.5 bar (15 m head) for domestic use.
  • 1.0 bar (10 m head) for fire hydrants.

Tank sizing for communities:

  • Daily demand: 20–100 L/person/day (WHO Sphere recommends 20 L/person/day).
  • Tanks should hold 12–24 hours of average demand.
  • Example: For 500 people at 50 L/day, total demand is 25 m³, requiring a 25 m³ tank.

Construction: Use reinforced concrete or HDPE tanks elevated on stands. Position tanks on hills to minimize structure height while maximizing pressure. The hydraulic grade line (water surface elevation) must exceed the highest point of distribution.

Preventing Water Hammer

Water hammer occurs when flow is abruptly stopped, causing pressure surges that can rupture pipes. The pressure rise is calculated as:

$$\Delta P = \rho c \Delta V$$

Where:

  • $\rho$ = water density (1,000 kg/m³)
  • $c$ = wave speed (1,200–1,400 m/s in steel, 300–500 m/s in PVC)
  • $\Delta V$ = change in velocity (e.g., 2 m/s for steel pipes)

Example: A steel pipe flowing at 2 m/s with sudden valve closure generates a 26 bar pressure spike. This can damage infrastructure.

Prevention:

  • Use slow-closing valves (e.g., gate valves).
  • Install surge tanks or air vessels.
  • Limit main flow velocities to 1.5 m/s.

Pump Selection and Operation

Centrifugal pumps are the most common type. Key factors:

  • Head-capacity curve: Pump head decreases with higher flow rates. The operating point is where the pump curve intersects the system head curve (static head + friction losses).
  • Best Efficiency Point (BEP): Operate near BEP to avoid energy waste and wear.
  • Specific speed (Ns): Low Ns (<1000) indicates high head, low flow; high Ns (>3000) means low head, high flow.

Rope pumps are ideal for hand-powered shallow wells (<15 m). They use a rope with rubber discs to lift water. Parts include a ropewheel, guide wheel, 25 mm rope, and rubber discs. Capacity: 30–40 L/min at 1.5 m/s rope speed. These pumps are fully repairable with local materials.

Solar Pump Sizing

Solar pumps require careful calculation to match energy needs. Steps: 1. Determine daily water demand (e.g., 5 m³/day). 2. Calculate total dynamic head (TDH) = static head + drawdown + friction losses. 3. Hydraulic power: $P_{hyd} = \rho g Q H$ (e.g., 1000 × 9.81 × (5/86400) × 50 = 28.4 W). 4. Account for system efficiency (40–60%): $P_{input} = P_{hyd} / \eta$. 5. Use peak sun hours (PSH) for location (e.g., 5 hours). 6. PV array size = $P_{input} \times (\text{total operating hours}/\text{PSH})$.

Example: For 5 m³/day, TDH 50 m, and 5 PSH:

  • $P_{hyd} = 28.4$ W
  • $P_{input} = 28.4 / 0.45 = 63$ W
  • PV needed = $63 \times (8/5) = 101$ W → install 150 W panel with 20% margin.

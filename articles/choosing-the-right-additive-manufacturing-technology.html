Choosing the Right Additive Manufacturing Technology

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: 3d-printing-additive-manufacturing-expert-depth.

Selecting the appropriate additive manufacturing (AM) technology depends on balancing factors like resolution, material options, cost, and scalability. The ISO/ASTM 52900 standard categorizes AM processes into seven types, each with distinct strengths and limitations. Understanding these differences ensures optimal results for specific tasks, from prototyping to industrial production.

Matching Technology to Application

1. Prototype Development and Iteration

Material extrusion (FDM/FFF) is ideal for rapid prototyping due to its low cost, ease of use, and diverse material library (PLA to PEEK). It offers design flexibility and quick turnaround times, making it perfect for iterative testing. However, its layer-by-layer approach may limit surface finish and mechanical strength compared to other methods.

2. Functional Parts with Mechanical Requirements

Powder bed fusion — both SLS and EBM — excels at producing parts with high mechanical strength. SLS uses laser sintering to fuse powdered materials, achieving properties close to injection-molded equivalents without support structures. EBM (electron beam melting) fuses metal powder with a focused electron beam in a vacuum, and is particularly suited for high-strength metal components and challenging alloys. Both avoid the need for support material, reducing post-processing effort.

3. High-Resolution End Products

Vat photopolymerization (SLA/DLP/MSLA) is the go-to choice for applications requiring micron-scale detail, such as jewelry, dental implants, and microfluidic devices. These technologies use light to cure liquid resins, delivering superior surface finish and precision. However, they often require careful post-curing and may have limited material options compared to other methods.

4. Production-Scale Manufacturing

For mass production, powder bed fusion (SLS) and metal-based systems like direct metal laser sintering (DMLS) offer a balance of speed, cost, and quality. These technologies can produce complex geometries while maintaining consistency. Injection molding molds created via AM further enable traditional manufacturing to benefit from additive design flexibility.

5. Large-Format Industrial Components

Directed energy deposition (WAAM) and binder jetting are suited for large-scale parts, such as aerospace components or construction elements. WAAM (wire arc additive manufacturing) deposits metal wire using MIG or TIG welding equipment, building near-net-shape parts layer by layer at high deposition rates. Binder jetting, which prints powder-bed structures using binding agents, offers scalability and cost efficiency for oversized projects.

Trade-Offs and Decision-Making

Each AM technology prioritizes different attributes. For example, FDM’s affordability contrasts with SLA’s precision, while SLS’s strength falls short of injection molding’s cost efficiency for high-volume runs. When selecting a method, consider:

  • Resolution: SLA/MSLA for fine details, FDM for rougher prototypes.
  • Material Diversity: FDM’s wide material range vs. SLS/EBM’s specialized metals.
  • Speed and Scalability: WAAM and binder jetting for large parts, SLS/DMLS for balanced production.
  • Post-Processing Needs: SLS/EBM’s minimal support waste versus SLA’s curing requirements.

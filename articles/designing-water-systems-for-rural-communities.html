Designing Water Systems for Rural Communities

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: water-purification-sanitation-systems-depth.

Calculating water needs, sizing storage, and selecting treatment methods are critical steps in creating sustainable off-grid water systems. Two real-world examples—village water supply and chlorine dosing for reservoirs—provide clear blueprints for practical design. Below is a step-by-step guide to replicating these systems, grounded in field-tested methods.

Calculating Water Demand

The first step in any water system is determining the total demand. For a village of 500 people, each requiring 50 liters per day for domestic use, the average daily demand is 25,000 liters (500 × 50 L). This translates to an average flow rate of 0.29 liters per second (25,000 L/day ÷ 86,400 seconds). However, peak hour demand is 3× the average, or 0.87 L/s, which must be considered for pipe sizing.

If the available water source exceeds this peak demand—such as a spring with a minimum flow of 4 L/s—the system can rely on gravity distribution. This ensures consistent pressure without pumps, reducing long-term maintenance.

Sizing Storage Tanks

Storage capacity must cover 24-hour demand, which for this example is 25 cubic meters (25,000 L). A reinforced concrete tank measuring 5 m × 5 m × 1.2 m provides 30 m³ of usable volume, exceeding the minimum requirement.

Placement is critical: the tank should be elevated on a hillside to provide at least 15 meters of head above the highest household. This gravity-fed system eliminates the need for pumps and ensures water flows naturally to taps.

Treatment Train for Spring Water

Spring water with turbidity 2–5 NTU and E. coli levels up to 50 CFU/100 mL requires a two-step treatment process. First, a slow sand filter (SSF) reduces turbidity to below 1 NTU and removes 2–4 log reductions in bacteria. Second, chlorination ensures residual disinfection.

To achieve a final E. coli level of 0 CFU/100 mL, apply 1.5 mg/L of chlorine after the SSF. This accounts for both the chlorine demand from the filter effluent (0.5–1.0 mg/L) and the residual target (0.5 mg/L). Using 5% bleach, this requires 0.75 liters per day, dosed via a drip chlorinator at the storage tank inlet.

Slow Sand Filter Sizing

Filter area is calculated using hydraulic loading rates. For a design flow of 0.29 L/s (or 1.55 m³/hr with buffer), a hydraulic loading rate of 0.15 m/hr (within the SSF operational range) requires 10.3 m² of filter area. A 4 m × 3 m filter (12 m²) with 1.0 m sand depth meets this, using sand with an effective size of 0.25 mm and a uniformity coefficient (UC) < 2.5.

Chlorine Dose for Reservoirs

When disinfecting a 50,000 L reservoir contaminated during construction, calculate the total chlorine dose by adding the demand for concrete (20 mg/L) and the desired residual (50 mg/L), totaling 70 mg/L. Using calcium hypochlorite (65% available chlorine), the required mass is 5.4 kg.

Add the granules to the reservoir, circulate for 30 minutes, and test the residual. Flush the system twice its volume to remove excess chlorine before refilling. Verify the residual returns to zero after flushing to ensure safety.

Digital Light Processing (DLP): A Guide to Micromirror-Based 3D Printing

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: 3d-printing-additive-manufacturing-expert-depth.

DLP technology leverages a Digital Micromirror Device (DMD) chipset, originally developed by Texas Instruments for projectors, to create high-resolution 3D prints. This system uses an array of 1.3 million microscopic mirrors—each 10–17 µm in pitch—to direct light onto a resin vat, curing layers in 2–10 seconds. The result is exceptional detail, with layer thicknesses of 5–20 µm and feature resolution down to 25–50 µm.

How DLP Works: The Micromirror Core

At the heart of DLP is the DMD chipset, a CMOS-based array of tilting mirrors that control light exposure. Each mirror tilts ±12° independently, directing light either toward the resin vat (to cure a layer) or away (to skip it). This whole-layer illumination approach is faster than scanning lasers, enabling rapid curing. The system’s precision—19–35 µm pixel size depending on optics—makes it ideal for applications requiring fine detail.

Why DLP Stands Out

DLP excels in applications where resolution and accuracy are paramount. Its ability to cure entire layers at once reduces print time compared to sequential methods. For instance, dental applications like crowns and bridges benefit from ISO 10993 biocompatible resins, while jewelry makers use it to craft intricate master patterns for investment casting. Microfluidic devices and micro-optical components also rely on DLP’s precision to achieve sub-50 µm features.

Limitations to Consider

Despite its strengths, DLP has notable drawbacks. Pixel stepping artifacts can occur on fine curved edges, leading to visible stair-stepping in complex geometries. The system is also limited to 405–385 nm UV wavelengths, requiring proprietary photoinitiators for optimal curing. Equipment costs range from $8,000 to $25,000 for professional systems, and build platforms are relatively small, typically 52 × 30 × 75 mm. These constraints may make DLP less suitable for large-scale or highly organic designs.

Where DLP Makes the Most Sense

DLP’s precision and speed make it a top choice for specialized fields. In dentistry, it produces custom prosthetics with high biocompatibility. Jewelry artisans use it to create master patterns for casting, ensuring intricate designs are replicated accurately. Microfluidic devices, which require tiny channels for fluid transport, also benefit from DLP’s sub-50 µm resolution. Even archaeological applications leverage DLP to replicate fragile artifacts with forensic detail, preserving historical accuracy.

Gear That Helps

  • A DMD chipset with 1.3 million mirrors
  • A resin vat compatible with 405–385 nm UV light
  • A UV light source with adjustable intensity
  • A build platform sized for 52 × 30 × 75 mm prints
  • Photoinitiators tailored to 405–385 nm wavelengths

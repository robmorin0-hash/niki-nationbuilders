Selective Laser Sintering (SLS): How It Works and When to Use It

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: 3d-printing-additive-manufacturing-expert-depth.

Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) is a powder-bed fusion process that uses a CO2 laser to fuse polyamide powders into functional parts. Unlike FDM, SLS eliminates the need for support structures by using unsintered powder as a natural scaffold, enabling complex geometries and isotropic mechanical properties. This guide distills the core principles, materials, equipment, and design rules of SLS to help you apply it effectively in off-grid or self-reliant contexts.

Process and Laser Parameters

SLS operates through a sequence of precise steps: a powder cartridge spreads a layer of powder across a heated bed (165–175°C for nylon), a laser scanner fuses the powder along the part’s cross-section, and a build platform lowers incrementally (80–150 µm layer height). A recoater blade replenishes the powder bed before repeating the cycle. Post-printing, parts are cooled slowly to prevent distortion, and unsintered powder is sifted and recycled (50–70% reuse rate).

Laser parameters directly affect print quality and speed. Power ranges from 30–100 W: higher power reduces print time but risks lower resolution, while lower power enables finer detail. Scan speed (1–3 m/s) must balance with power to maintain consistent energy input. Spot size (100–150 µm) determines surface finish—larger spots speed up printing but create coarser textures, while smaller spots improve detail. Layer thickness (80–150 µm) trades off resolution (thinner layers yield higher detail) against print time (thinner layers take 3× longer).

Material Properties and Costs

Nylon 12 (PA12) is the most common SLS material, offering tensile strength of 48–65 MPa, elongation at break of 15–25%, and excellent resistance to hydrocarbons and oils. Its 1.6% water absorption requires conditioning for dimensional stability. At $80–120/kg raw material, PA12 costs $3–8 per part including overhead. Nylon 11 (PA11), a bio-sourced alternative, has lower tensile strength (40–50 MPa) but better flexibility and slightly lower water absorption (1.5%).

Composite powders enhance mechanical properties: 10–30% glass fiber reinforcement boosts tensile strength to 50–80 MPa, while carbon-fiber blends reach 70–100 MPa with lower density. These materials improve thermal conductivity, enabling applications like heat sinks, but cost 50–100% more than unfilled powders.

Equipment Landscape

Desktop SLS systems are emerging at $5–25K, with the Sintratec Kit standing out as an open-source, ~$8K option. It uses a 30 W CO2 laser and has a 100 × 100 × 75 mm build volume, ideal for prototyping and customization. Professional-grade desktop systems run $15–20K, offering reliable powder recycling and a roughly 100 × 100 × 100 mm build space. The Formlabs Fuse 1+ at around $25K includes closed-loop powder recycling and integrated heating/cooling.

Industrial SLS systems from Eos or 3D Systems scale to 250 × 250 × 300 mm+ build volumes, using 50–100 W CO2 lasers and 80+ µm layers. These machines, priced $200K–500K+, support continuous 24/7 production and offer nylon 12, 11, TPU, and glass-filled material options.

Design Considerations

SLS design requires attention to geometric and material specifics. Minimum wall thickness should be 0.7–0.8 mm to avoid powder entrapment and structural weakness. Enclosed cavities must include 2–3 mm diameter drainage holes for post-printing powder removal. Surface finish is inherently rough (Ra 6–12 µm), though vapor smoothing or bead blasting can refine it.

Anisotropy is negligible in SLS, with XY and Z-axis strength equivalent—unlike FDM’s layer-dependent weakness. Powder recycling economics is critical: 50–70% virgin powder addition per cycle drives costs, so optimizing reuse rates is essential for sustainability and cost control.

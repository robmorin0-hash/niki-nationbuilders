How to Assess Solar Resources for Your Off-Grid System

Grounded in primary-source research. Source: solar-pv-systems-design-installation-depth.

Solar resource assessment is the foundation of any reliable off-grid photovoltaic (PV) system. Accurate data on irradiance levels, seasonal variation, and local weather patterns ensures your system is sized correctly for energy production. This guide provides actionable steps to evaluate solar potential, select optimal system design, and avoid costly mistakes.

Understanding Solar Irradiance Metrics

Three key metrics define solar energy availability: Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI), Diffuse Horizontal Irradiance (DHI), and Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI). GHI measures total solar energy reaching a horizontal surface, peaking at ~1000 W/m² during clear skies. DHI represents scattered sunlight from clouds and atmospheric particles, contributing 10–15% of total irradiance on clear days and 100% on overcast days. DNI, the intensity of direct sunlight perpendicular to the sun, is critical for concentrated solar power systems but also influences fixed-tilt PV efficiency.

To estimate annual energy output, use Peak Sun Hours (PSH), which convert GHI into equivalent hours of full sunlight. For example, a location with 4.5 PSH receives the same energy as 4.5 hours of 1000 W/m² irradiance daily. PSH values vary widely: Canada averages 3.5–4.5 PSH, Alberta ranges from 4.0–4.5 PSH, and equatorial regions reach 5.0–5.5 PSH.

Accessing Regional Solar Data

Free online databases provide historical irradiance data to inform your design:

1. NSRDB (National Solar Radiation Database) by NREL: Offers 0.038° resolution data (4 km grids) for the US, Mexico, and Caribbean. Use the PVWatts tool to simulate energy production based on location-specific irradiance. 2. PVGIS (Photovoltaic Geographical Information System) by the EU Commission: 1 km grid resolution covering Europe, Africa, and the Middle East (with limited North America), built on MERRA-2 satellite reanalysis. Provides raster maps, time-series export, and system-sizing calculations. 3. Solargis: Global coverage (180 countries) at 250 m resolution — the highest of these three. A commercial tool (the free tier is limited; paid subscriptions run $500–5,000/year) that integrates with design software such as PVsyst and HOMER Pro.

Compare data from multiple sources to account for local microclimates and seasonal variations.

Selecting the Right System Design

Optimize your PV setup by considering local irradiance patterns and installation constraints:

  • Tilt Angle: Match panel tilt to your latitude for balanced year-round output — about 53° in Alberta. To favor winter generation in a northern climate, add ~15° (latitude + 15°, roughly 68° in Alberta) at a ~5% summer penalty, or use a two-position seasonal adjustment (~25° summer / ~55° winter) for about a 15% annual gain over fixed tilt.
  • Shading Analysis: Use tools like Solmetric SunEye (or Google Earth Pro shadow analysis) to identify shading from trees or buildings. A single tree's shadow typically costs 5–15% of annual yield, and saplings mature to 50+ ft over a system's lifetime — so plan around 30+ year growth.
  • Tracking Systems: Single-axis trackers can boost annual energy capture by 25–35%, but they add cost (roughly $100–200/kW) and mechanical complexity.

Prioritize system reliability over marginal efficiency gains, especially in areas with high cloud cover or variable weather.

Gear That Helps

  • An MPPT charge controller to maximize energy transfer from panels to batteries.
  • A solar panel array (monocrystalline panels are efficient for most climates).
  • A solar tracking system (single-axis) for high-irradiance regions.
  • A solar racking system designed for local wind loads and panel mounting.
